# Eating Disorder Coping Strategies

Living with an eating disorder presents unique challenges, but with the right coping strategies and support, recovery is possible. Whether you're managing an eating disorder yourself or supporting a loved one, understanding effective coping mechanisms can make a significant difference in the recovery journey.

## Understanding Your Treatment Options

Eating disorders affect everyone differently and often range in severity, so there is no one-size-fits-all approach to treatment. Understanding your treatment options can help reduce anxiety and uncertainty about the recovery process.

Treatment typically includes a combination of:

- **Psychotherapy**: Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) is a first-line treatment that helps people change thought patterns contributing to their condition and develop new coping skills. Family-based treatment (FBT) is particularly recommended for teens with anorexia and bulimia.

- **Nutritional Therapy**: Working with a registered dietitian who specializes in eating disorders can help challenge rigid thoughts around food, body, and weight. They provide accurate nutritional information and can create personalized meal plans that support recovery.

- **Medical Treatment**: Medical monitoring may be necessary to address health issues stemming from eating disorders, including cardiovascular or gastrointestinal concerns.

- **Medication**: While there aren't specific medications for eating disorders, medications can help manage coexisting conditions like depression or anxiety.

- **Higher Levels of Care**: Depending on your needs, this might include partial hospitalization (day treatment) or inpatient residential treatment.

## Practice Self-Compassion and Positive Self-Talk

Research shows that self-talk plays a critical role in how eating disorders develop and persist. Dysfunctional and negative self-talk is associated with increased symptoms and severity of eating disorders.

People with eating disorders often struggle with perfectionist tendencies and harsh self-criticism. However, self-criticism won't help motivate you or support recovery—in fact, it can increase feelings of shame and negative emotions.

To combat negative self-talk:

- Notice when your inner critic becomes active and consciously work to stay positive
- Use affirmation exercises to challenge self-critical thoughts
- Practice self-compassion, particularly regarding body image
- Remember that recovery takes time—avoid expecting immediate results
- Reframe negative thoughts in more realistic, positive ways

Research indicates that people with eating disorders who show themselves compassion, especially concerning body image, may experience reduced disordered eating behaviors compared to those who don't practice self-compassion.

## Build and Maintain Your Support System

Social support positively contributes to recovery from eating disorders. Research shows that social connections are critical to successful eating disorder recovery. You shouldn't have to go through this alone.

### Reaching Out for Help

Asking for help can be a daily process. It may feel tough to let someone know you have an eating disorder, but you can start small:

- Meet up with a friend or catch up over the phone
- You don't need to provide all the details immediately—simply let trusted people know you're going through a difficult time and could use support
- Be specific about what kind of support you need (such as support during meals)
- Confide in people who have earned your trust

Keeping secrets about difficult experiences can lead to feelings of shame and prevent you from getting the support you need. When trusted friends and family know what's going on, they're better equipped to help.

### Working With Professionals

People with eating disorders are more likely to recover with a specialized treatment team. In most cases, willpower and self-help cannot replace the professional guidance of a therapist, dietitian, and physician. These professionals have years of training and experience to support your recovery.

Listen to your treatment team when they recommend specific changes, even when it seems scary. Changes like adding medication, adopting a meal plan, or considering a higher level of care are often important and necessary steps.

## Engage in Self-Care Activities

Make sure you're doing things you enjoy as much as possible. Self-care can reduce stress, depression, and anxiety. Effective self-care strategies include:

- **Prioritize enjoyable activities**: Watch favorite movies, visit favorite places, or engage in hobbies
- **Use relaxation techniques**: Practice visualization, deep breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, or other stress management strategies
- **Disrupt triggering situations**: If you feel stressed and concerned about triggers, disrupt the pattern with an activity like taking a quick walk. Doing anything that takes you out of your thoughts can help calm your nervous system
- **Practice gratitude**: Keep a gratitude journal to stay motivated and inspired
- **Set boundaries**: Determine how much you can give to others while protecting your own recovery

## Celebrate Your Progress

Eating disorder recovery is not a linear path. There are often setbacks, and progress may vary. Full recovery can take months or even years, and many people continue working on recovery throughout their lives.

- Reward yourself for any progress you make in recovery
- Recovery is hard work—celebrate yourself for every step forward
- Be patient with yourself through ups and downs
- Don't feel shame about slips or relapses—they're a normal part of the journey
- Check in with your treatment team if you aren't making the progress you hoped for

## Stick to Your Recovery Plan

Even when tempted to stray from your therapy or eating plan, focus on sticking with it. It's normal to feel uncomfortable at times during recovery—discomfort often signals growth and change.

- Follow your meal plan consistently
- Attend therapy sessions regularly
- Take prescribed medications as directed
- Talk openly with your doctor or therapist about difficulties you're experiencing
- Face situations you've been avoiding, such as eating certain foods or tolerating feelings of fullness

## Face Your Fears Gradually

Recovery requires facing situations that you may have been avoiding. Work with your treatment team to develop a plan to gradually face these fears in a safe, supported way. This might include:

- Eating previously feared foods
- Tolerating feelings of fullness
- Managing anxiety without using eating disorder behaviors
- Reducing excessive exercise behaviors

## Stay Optimistic and Believe in Recovery

Eating disorders are serious conditions, but they are treatable, and full recovery is possible. Self-acceptance and positive relationships are associated with improved eating disorder recovery outcomes.

Remember:

- You are worth the time and financial commitment that treatment requires
- Recovery is possible—many people achieve full recovery
- When you begin to lose hope, talk to your therapist right away
- Stay positive and seek encouragement from trusted loved ones

## Additional Support Resources

If accessing professional treatment is difficult:

- Online eating disorder support groups can provide community and understanding
- Helplines offer information and crisis support
- Self-help interventions, particularly for bulimia and binge eating disorder, may be beneficial when professional care isn't accessible
- Research shows that automated online self-help interventions can be effective tools for reducing eating disorder symptoms

Remember that while eating disorders can be distressing and uncomfortable, with appropriate support, coping strategies, and treatment, recovery is absolutely achievable. Take it one day at a time, be kind to yourself, and trust in the recovery process.
