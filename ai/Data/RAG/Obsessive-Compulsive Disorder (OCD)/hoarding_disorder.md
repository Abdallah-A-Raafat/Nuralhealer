# Hoarding Disorder

## Overview
- Hoarding disorder is characterized by **difficulty discarding possessions**, even those of little or no value.
- Accumulated items clutter living spaces, making them unusable and potentially unsafe.
- Can include **excessive acquisition**, actively seeking more unneeded items.

---

## Types of Hoarding
- **Animal hoarding:** Acquiring more animals than one can care for.
- **Compulsive shopping:** Preoccupation with buying unneeded items.
- **Object hoarding:** Accumulation of specific items like paper, books, clothing, or trash.

---

## Symptoms
- Inability to discard possessions.
- Varying levels of **insight** into behavior.
- Poor insight: Unaware of severity, even in unsafe conditions.
- Excessive acquisition behaviors.

### Potential Risks
- Fire hazards and blocked exits.
- Unsanitary conditions: rotting food, garbage, and pet waste.
- Disease risks from pets or poor hygiene.
- Impaired daily functioning: food prep, hygiene, social interaction.

---

## Diagnosis
- Classified under **Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders** in DSM-5.
- Criteria include:
  - Persistent difficulty discarding possessions
  - Distress associated with discarding
  - Clutter that compromises living space
  - Clinically significant impairment
  - Not attributable to another medical or psychiatric disorder

---

## Causes and Risk Factors
- **Genetic and family history:** Family members with hoarding disorder
- **Environmental factors:** Trauma, significant life stressors
- **Behavioral tendencies:** Compulsive buying, indecisiveness
- Often co-occurs with **depression and anxiety** (~75% of cases)

---

## Prevalence
- Estimated 2–6% of the U.S. population
- Typically begins in childhood/adolescence and worsens over time
- Daily functioning declines as clutter accumulates

---

## Treatment

### Psychotherapy
- **Hoarding-specific CBT**: Change thought patterns, decision-making, and organization skills
- Process includes:
  - Functional assessment
  - Psychoeducation
  - Goal-setting with clinician
  - Cognitive restructuring
  - Skills training (organization, problem-solving)
  - Behavioral experiments (exposure, decluttering)

### Medications
- No FDA-approved drugs specifically for hoarding
- **SSRIs/SNRIs** may help when co-occurring with OCD or depression
- Other options under investigation: cognitive enhancers, psychostimulants

---

## Coping Strategies
- **Seek support:** Friends, family, or support groups
- **Practice self-care:** Nutrition, hygiene, and accessible living areas
- **Focus on small steps:** Break tasks into achievable goals
- **Accept help:** Professional organizers or cleaning services

- Contact **SAMHSA National Helpline** at 1-800-662-4357 for resources

---

## Key Takeaways
- Hoarding disorder makes discarding items difficult, regardless of value.
- Accumulation can make living spaces unsafe or uninhabitable.
- Treatment is complex, requiring psychotherapy, possibly medication, and practical support.
