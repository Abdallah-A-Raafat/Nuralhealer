Living with both anxiety and depression can significantly impact your life, making everyday tasks feel overwhelming. Here are some tips on how to cope.

Anxiety and depression are two of the most common mental health conditions, affecting millions worldwide. These conditions often show up together, sharing symptoms like fatigue, worry, and trouble focusing.

Managing both conditions can be complicated, so using comprehensive approaches is key. Let’s delve into the connection between these disorders and explore coping strategies if you’re managing both.

The link between anxiety and depression
Anxiety and depression often go hand in hand. Around 46% of individuals with lifetime major depressive disorder have also faced at least one anxiety disorder. Their combined presence often leads to more intense symptoms and can complicate therapy.

Anxiety often appears first, possibly paving the way for later experiences with depression. People with depression also frequently grapple with significant anxiety symptoms, resulting in what’s called “anxious depression.”

Both anxiety and depression can be influenced by similar genetic predispositions and tough experiences in life, such as trauma or stressful events. These factors can increase the likelihood of someone developing either or both conditions.

In addition, both anxiety and depression affect similar parts of the brain that handle emotions. They impact areas involved in regulating and processing emotions, which might explain why the symptoms and experiences between the two conditions often overlap and coexist.

What causes depression and anxiety?
There’s also a significant overlap in the risk factors for both anxiety and depression. These may include:

Genetics and family history: Having relatives with anxiety or depression increases the risk. Research spanning decades suggests that both anxiety and depression have a significant genetic component, with estimates of heritability ranging from 30%-40%.
Trauma and stressful life events: Experiencing trauma, loss, abuse, or significant stress can trigger either condition. An older study from 2014 indicates there’s a link between experiences of childhood abuse and mood disorders (including depression), anxiety, and substance misuse disorders.
Environmental factors: Stressors like work, relationships, or financial problems can contribute to both anxiety and depression. A study of nearly 5,000 adolescents in China found that anxiety significantly predicted depression both initially and over time. Notably, as anxiety decreased, so did depressive symptoms over the same period.
Personality traits: Certain traits, like excessive worrying, low self-esteem, or pessimism, can increase vulnerability to both conditions. 2023 research suggests that neuroticism is the most significant risk trait for anxiety and depressive symptoms.
Brain chemistry and structure: Imbalances in neurotransmitters and changes in brain structure may contribute to both anxiety and depression. A 2017 review suggests that the dopamine disturbances frequently observed in depression likely originate from disruptions in the brain circuits controlling dopamine activity.
Substance misuse: Alcohol, drugs, and caffeine abuse can worsen or trigger symptoms of anxiety or depression. Research from 2020 suggests that anxiety and depression are commonly seen together in people with substance use disorders.
Chronic health conditions: Long-term illnesses or ongoing health issues may lead to both anxiety and depression. A study of 86 hospitalized patients with pulmonary tuberculosis in Brazil found that 37% met the criteria for depression while 38% had symptoms of anxiety.
Hormonal changes: Studies indicate that women are twice as prone to experiencing depression and anxiety. Fluctuating sex hormones, particularly estrogen, play a crucial role in this heightened risk.

How to cope with anxiety and depression 
Here are some tips for coping with both anxiety and depression:

1. Practice mindfulness and relaxation techniques
Mindfulness techniques help reduce stress and promote a sense of calmness by focusing your attention on the present moment, easing anxiety and boosting mood.

Consider engaging in activities that promote relaxation, like deep breathing, meditation, or yoga. For example, spend 10 minutes each morning doing guided breathing exercises or practice mindful walking in nature.

2. Regular exercise
Physical activity releases endorphins, the body’s natural mood lifters, and helps reduce stress hormones, aiding in managing symptoms of depression and anxiety. Aim for activities you enjoy, whether it’s:

a daily walk
cycling
dancing
joining a fitness class
Even short bursts of activity can make a difference.

A 2022 review of diverse populations found that physical activity has moderate effects in reducing symptoms of depression, anxiety, and distress compared to usual support.

The greatest improvements were seen in individuals with:

depression
HIV
kidney disease
pregnant and postpartum women
healthy individuals
Notably, higher-intensity physical activity was linked to greater symptom improvements. But the effectiveness of physical activity interventions slightly diminished with longer durations.

3. Maintain a supportive social network
Connecting with others, including friends, family, or support groups, can provide emotional support, reduce feelings of isolation, and offer a sense of belonging, which is crucial for mental well-being.

Consider scheduling regular calls or meetups with others, whether in person or virtually. Join clubs or community activities to foster new connections.

4. Healthy habits
Eating well, getting enough sleep, and keeping a regular routine can help support your overall health, impacting mood, energy levels, and cognitive function.

Try incorporating more fruits, vegetables, healthy fats, and whole grains into your meals, and establish a consistent sleep routine.


Next steps
Having both depression and anxiety can significantly impact your life, intensifying feelings of hopelessness and agitation. It can make everyday tasks feel overwhelming, but utilizing various coping strategies and seeking support can help you navigate these experiences effectively.
