There are several types of depression, each with specific causes, symptoms, and timelines. Whatever type you have, help is available.

Depression is not only hard to endure, it is also a risk factor for heart disease and dementia. “Depressive symptoms can occur in adults for many reasons. If you are experiencing mood or cognitive changes that last for more than a few weeks, it's a good idea to bring this up with your doctor or consult a mental health specialist to help sort out possible causes,” says Dr. Nancy Donovan, an instructor in psychiatry at Harvard Medical School. The four most common types of depression are major depression, persistent depressive disorder (formerly known as dysthymia), bipolar disorder, and seasonal affective disorder.

The following are some of the common types of depression:

The type of depression you’re diagnosed with will depend on your specific symptoms, when they arise, and how long they last.

Depression symptoms, in general, can affect your daily life, relationships, and work or school life.

Major depressive disorder

Many times, when people say they have depression, it’s major depressive disorder (MDD). MDD — sometimes known as clinical depression — can cause you to experience:

a consistently low mood

sleep disturbances

decreased interest in activities

lack of energy

guilt

changes in movement patterns (moving slower or faster than usual)

changes in appetite

worthlessness

thoughts of suicide, self-harm, and death

According to the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th Edition, Text Revision (DSM-5-TR), to receive a diagnosis of major depressive disorder you need to have at least 5 of the 9 symptoms above for at least 2 weeks.

One of those symptoms needs to be a low mood or low interest in activities that negatively affect your social and work life.

Major depression looks different in different people.

A doctor can help determine if your symptoms are related to major depression, or if they result from conditions, including other types of depression. For example, if you also have periods of mania, a diagnosis of bipolar disorder, not depression, is possible.

Atypical depression

Atypical depression (also called atypical depressive disorder) can be an additional feature or type of major depression or dysthymic disorder (a mild, long-lasting form of depression). People with atypical depression often have their first symptoms at an early age, during their teenage years.

Atypical depression is common despite its name (in this case, "atypical" means different from "typical" depression). It's different from another type of depression called melancholic depression.

One of the main differences between atypical depression and melancholic depression is how your mood reacts to good things. With atypical depression, your mood often gets better when something positive happens.

To diagnose atypical depression, a person must also have at least two of these symptoms along with mood reactivity:

sleeping too much

eating more or gaining weight

being extra sensitive to rejection, which can cause problems in relationships and work

a mental feeling of heaviness or being weighed down

Persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia)

Persistent depressive disorder (PDD) was originally believed to be a personality disorder, but the DSM-5-TR now classifies it as a type of depressive disorder.

To receive a diagnosis of persistent depressive disorder, you need to experience at least 2 symptoms of depression for at least 2 years, with no more than 2 months without symptoms. PDD only needs to be present for 1 year in people under age 18.

Symptoms of persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia) may include long-lasting low mood, low energy, low self-esteem, difficulty concentrating, changes in sleep or appetite, and feelings of hopelessness.

About 3% of U.S. people have persistent depressive disorder.

Many people with PDD feel like this is just “how they are,” but you can always benefit from speaking with a doctor about how you’re feeling. They can help you find a treatment plan and care that helps you feel better.

Premenstrual dysphoric disorder

Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD) is a depressive disorder that’s similar to premenstrual syndrome (PMS). The difference is the severity of symptoms.

While many people who menstruate will have PMS, not everyone will develop PMDD. This type of depression most likely occurs due to dropping hormone levels following ovulation.

In order for a doctor to diagnose PMDD, they’ll want to know if you have depressive symptoms most of your menstrual cycles, and if these usually start within a week before menstruation starts.

According to the DSM-5-TR, for a diagnosis of PMDD, at least 4 of the following must be present:

strong, variable emotions, known as affective lability

increased irritability, anger, or conflict with others

a depressed mood, feelings of hopelessness, or self-deprecating thoughts

anxiety, tension, or feeling on edge

The DSM-5-TR also requires that 5 of the following symptoms are also present:

reduced interest in your usual activities

difficulty thinking or concentrating

tiredness or fatigue

changes in appetite or eating habits

sleep problems

feeling overwhelmed or out of control

physical symptoms, such as bloating, breast tenderness, or weight gain

About 5% of women in their childbearing years experience this condition.

Seasonal affective disorder

While clinically known as major depressive disorder with seasonal patterns, many people still know this type of depression as seasonal affective disorder (SAD).

SAD is a type of major depressive disorder, meaning that it involves the same symptoms, it’s just that symptoms only occur during certain seasons, rather than throughout the year.

A diagnosis of seasonal depression is based on having:

symptoms of major depression

episodes that occur during specific seasons for at least 2 consecutive years

episodes that are more frequent than other depressive episodes

About 5% of U.S. adults experience seasonal depression.

Bipolar disorder

Formerly known as manic depression or manic-depressive illness, bipolar disorder is not actually considered a type of depression or depressive condition.

People with bipolar disorder may experience depressive episodes. The symptoms of these are the same as episodes in major depressive disorder.

Postpartum depression

If depression, emptiness, or hopelessness doesn’t go away within 2 weeks after giving birth, you may be experiencing postpartum depression, also known as major depressive disorder with peripartum onset.

Symptoms of postpartum depression include:

feeling hopeless, overwhelmed, or sad after giving birth

not feeling connected to the baby

low energy

crying frequently

changes in eating habits

problems with memory or decision making

thoughts of harming your baby or yourself

About 1 in 9 people experience postpartum depression after giving birth for the first time.

Treatments

Some treatment options include:

psychotherapy or counseling

antidepressants

in severe cases, hospitalization
